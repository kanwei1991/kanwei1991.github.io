One of my favorite ways to introduce momentum is with a simple marble collision activity. It's low-cost, visual, and gets students making predictions before we even write down equations.

## The Setup

You'll need:
- A few marbles of different sizes
- A flat ruler with a groove (or a track)
- A piece of tape to mark positions

## The Activity

1. Place one marble at the center of the ruler.
2. Roll another marble toward it from the left.
3. Students predict what will happen before each trial.
4. Try different combinations: same size, different sizes, two marbles vs. one.

## The "Aha" Moment

When a small marble hits a larger one, students see that the smaller marble bounces back while the larger one rolls forward slowly. That's when the concept of momentum clicks — it's not just about speed, and it's not just about mass. It's about *both*.

## Connecting to the Math

After the activity, we derive:

$$p = mv$$

and

$$m_1 v_{1i} + m_2 v_{2i} = m_1 v_{1f} + m_2 v_{2f}$$

By this point, students already *feel* why conservation of momentum makes sense — they've seen it in action. The math just formalizes what they observed.
