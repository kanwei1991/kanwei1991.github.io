## Objective

To verify Newton's Second Law of Motion: $F = ma$, where the net force applied to an object is equal to its mass multiplied by its acceleration.

## Theory

According to Newton's Second Law:

$$F_{\text{net}} = ma$$

In this experiment, a cart on a nearly frictionless track is pulled by a hanging mass. The tension in the string provides the net force. By varying the hanging mass and measuring the resulting acceleration, we can verify the linear relationship between force and acceleration.

## Procedure

1. Measure and record the mass of the dynamics cart.
2. Set up the track with the pulley at one end.
3. Connect the string from the cart over the pulley to the hanging mass holder.
4. Place the motion sensor at the opposite end of the track.
5. Start with a small hanging mass (e.g., 5 g).
6. Release the cart and record the acceleration using the motion sensor.
7. Repeat steps 5–6 with increasing hanging masses (10 g, 15 g, 20 g, ...).
8. Record all data in a table.

## Data Analysis

1. Calculate the net force for each trial using $F = mg$.
2. Plot a graph of $F$ vs $a$.
3. The slope of the best-fit line should equal the total mass of the system (cart + hanging mass).

## Conclusion

If the graph of force vs. acceleration is linear and passes through the origin, Newton's Second Law is verified within experimental uncertainty. Discuss sources of error and how they could be minimized.
